Two decades of waterborne pathogen science

From my doctoral work at NUS to postdoctoral research at Singapore-MIT, I have spent over a decade developing and applying qPCR-based methods for the detection, source-tracking, and inactivation of microbial pathogens in natural waters — including reservoirs, rainwater, and urban water systems.

At the Singapore-Delft Water Alliance, I contributed to early-warning systems for Singapore's Upper Peirce Reservoir. At SMART (Singapore-MIT Alliance), I led a research team studying cyanobacteria and cyanotoxin dynamics in reservoir waters, applying next-generation sequencing to understand how microbial communities behave during algal bloom events — directly informing Singapore's water security infrastructure.

Earlier, at Unilever Research, I was the microbiological lead for the development of Pureit® — India's first gravity-based home water purifier — establishing pathogen log-removal benchmarks and setting up virology and parasitology lab facilities to USEPA standards.

Shaping global standards for non-sewered sanitation

As Principal Microbiologist at TÜV SÜD PSB under the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation's sanitation programme, I worked at the front line of translating science into international policy. I represented the Singapore Standards Council as an expert microbiologist on the ISO Technical Committees responsible for ISO 30500 — the global standard for non-sewered sanitation system requirements and test methods — and ISO 31800, engaging with government delegations, manufacturers, and testing laboratories across more than 15 countries.

Beyond the standards themselves, I led the design and establishment of environmental testing infrastructure in India, Senegal, South Africa, and China, and co-authored interim standards for compost derived from faecal sludge treatment — building the circular economy case for resource recovery from sanitation.

I also developed a digital training platform for manufacturers, testing laboratories, and regulators — making the new ISO standards accessible to the global WASH community in a practical, self-paced format.

Building sustainability from the ground up

At Ashoka University, I founded and led India's first Office of Sustainability at a liberal arts university — designing it from zero into a functioning, cross-institutional body that integrated sustainability across operations, academics, and community engagement. Within two years, the university achieved a debut QS World University Sustainability ranking (globally 1501; rank 900 in SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities).

Key achievements include pioneering India's first university-led menstrual waste recycling programme, campus-wide water and waste management systems, the university's first biodiversity guide, annual SDG Expo programming, and institutionalised sustainability reporting aligned with SDG frameworks.

Through Ashoka's partnership with DRIIV (an initiative of the Office of the Principal Scientific Adviser to the Government of India), I also co-developed and delivered sustainability education modules for national teacher training programmes — contributing to GoI capacity-building at scale.

Making research matter beyond the laboratory

Across my career, I have consistently worked at the boundary between scientific evidence and public decision-making. This means not just producing research, but ensuring it reaches the people who need to act on it — whether that is a government delegation negotiating an ISO standard, a university leadership team making sustainability decisions, or a regulator in a low-income country evaluating a new sanitation technology.

My science communication work spans peer-reviewed publications, policy briefs, digital training courses, conference keynotes, and sustainability reporting. I am currently completing the Women Independent Director Leadership Programme at IIM Ahmedabad — adding board governance to the range of contexts in which I can bring evidence-based thinking to consequential decisions.
